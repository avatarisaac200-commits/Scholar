§ 1 — Viruses

Characteristics of Viruses

Viruses occupy a uniquely controversial position in biology — they are considered non-living acellular pathogens because they lack the hallmarks of life independently. They cannot grow, metabolise food, or reproduce on their own. Only inside a host cell do they spring into action, hijacking cellular machinery to replicate. Understanding what makes a virus a virus is foundational to everything else in microbiology.

Core Definition
A virus is a non-living, acellular obligate intracellular parasite consisting of genetic material (DNA or RNA, never both) enclosed in a protein coat (capsid), sometimes surrounded by a lipid envelope. It can only replicate inside a living host cell.

The 11 Key Characteristics

1. Acellular
Viruses lack all cellular structures — no cytoplasm, no nucleus, no organelles. They are biological particles, not true cells.
2. No Independent Metabolism
They cannot convert food to energy, respire, or grow independently. 100% dependent on host cell biochemistry.
3. Genetic Material
Contains either DNA or RNA — never both. Can be single-stranded or double-stranded, linear or circular, positive-sense or negative-sense.
DNA = instruction manual · RNA = ready-to-read translation
4. Capsid (Protein Coat)
A protective shell made of protein subunits called capsomeres. It protects the genetic material and determines viral shape.
5. Envelope
A lipoprotein layer surrounding the capsid in many (not all) viruses, derived from the host cell membrane. Enveloped viruses are more sensitive to detergents.
HIV, Influenza = enveloped · Poliovirus = non-enveloped (naked)
6. Obligate Intracellular Parasite
Must infect a living host cell to replicate. Outside a host, they are inert particles — neither alive nor dead in the conventional sense.
7. No Metabolism
They don't respire, produce energy, or synthesise food. Considered non-living for this reason.
8. Ultramicroscopic Size
About 100–1000× smaller than human cells. The smallest viruses are ~2000× smaller than a grain of sand. Visualised only by electron microscopy.
9. Host Specificity
Each virus infects only specific host types and specific cell types within that host — determined by surface receptor compatibility.
HIV infects CD4+ T-cells · Rabies targets nervous tissue
10. Varied Shapes
Icosahedral, helical, spherical, or complex (combining multiple shapes). Shape is determined by the capsid structure.
11. Structural Proteins
Structural proteins make up the capsid. Some viruses also have non-structural proteins (enzymes like reverse transcriptase, polymerases).

Viral Shapes at a Glance

Scientists classify viruses partly by morphology. The four main shapes and their examples:

💡 Sense/Antisense RNA — Exam Trap! Positive-sense (+) RNA can be directly used as mRNA by the host ribosome — no extra step needed. Negative-sense (−) RNA must first be converted to positive-sense by a viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) before protein synthesis can begin. Most DNA viruses are effectively positive-sense.
🎉 Fun Fact

The smallest known virus is Circovirus, at just ~17 nm — roughly 2,000× smaller than a grain of sand. The largest, Mimivirus, is so big it can be seen under a light microscope and was initially mistaken for a bacterium!

✎ Quick Check 1.1

Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of viruses?

Viruses CANNOT reproduce independently — they are obligate intracellular parasites that require a living host cell to replicate. This is one of the key reasons they are considered non-living.

✎ Quick Check 1.2

The protein coat surrounding the viral genetic material is called:

The capsid is the protein coat made of capsomeres. The envelope is a lipoprotein layer that SOME viruses have outside their capsid — derived from the host cell membrane. The nucleoid is a bacterial structure.
§ 2 — Viruses

Classification of Viruses

Viruses are classified on the basis of their phenotypic characteristics — physical features, core contents, chemical composition, capsid structure, size, shape, replication modes, and genome structure. The Baltimore classification system, organised by genetic material, is the most widely used today.

Classification by Host Range

🐾 Animal Viruses +

Viruses that infect by invading the cells of animals including humans. They must bind to specific cell surface receptors to gain entry.

Examples: Influenza virus, Mumps virus, Rabies virus, Poliovirus, Herpes simplex virus, HIV, Hepatitis B/C, Ebola virus, Coronavirus (SARS-CoV-2)

Clinical Link: Most human diseases caused by viruses fall in this category — from the common cold (Rhinovirus) to deadly haemorrhagic fevers (Ebolavirus). Vaccines target these viruses by priming the immune system to recognise capsid or envelope proteins.
🌿 Plant Viruses +

Infect plants by invading plant cells. Replication is obligate — only within host plant cells. Transmitted by insects (aphids, whiteflies), contaminated tools, or infected seeds.

Examples: Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV) — the first virus ever discovered (Ivanovsky, 1892), Potato Virus Y, Beet Yellow Virus, Turnip Yellow Virus, Cauliflower Mosaic Virus

Historical Note

TMV was the first entity described as a "filterable agent" — it passed through porcelain filters that trapped bacteria, leading to the recognition of viruses as a distinct category of infectious agent.

🧫 Bacteriophages (Phages) +

Viruses that specifically infect bacteria cells. They are the most abundant biological entities on Earth — estimated 10³¹ phages exist. They have a complex morphology with head (icosahedral), tail, and tail fibres.

Types: DNA virus (T4 bacteriophage), MV-11 virus, RNA virus (Qβ phage), λ-phage (lambda phage — key in molecular biology research)

Lytic vs Lysogenic Cycles: In the lytic cycle, the phage immediately replicates and lyses (bursts) the host bacterium. In the lysogenic cycle, the phage DNA integrates into the bacterial chromosome as a prophage and replicates along with the host — potentially remaining dormant for generations before activation.
🐛 Insect Viruses +

Viruses that infect insects — also called viral pathogens of insects. Considered powerful biocontrol agents in modern agriculture as alternatives to chemical pesticides.

Examples: Ascovirus, Entomopoxvirus, Baculoviruses (used commercially as bioinsecticides), Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus (NPV)

Classification by Replication Site

Replication LocationExamples
Cytoplasm onlyAll RNA viruses except Influenza virus
Nucleus AND cytoplasmInfluenza virus, Poxvirus
Nucleus onlyAll DNA viruses except Poxvirus
⚠ Common Exam Trap: Poxvirus is a DNA virus that replicates in the CYTOPLASM, not the nucleus — it is the exception to the rule that DNA viruses replicate in the nucleus. Similarly, Influenza is an RNA virus that replicates in both nucleus and cytoplasm.

✎ Quick Check 2.1

HIV belongs to which Baltimore classification group?

HIV is a Retrovirus (Group VI) — it carries positive-sense ssRNA plus reverse transcriptase enzyme, which converts its RNA genome into DNA. That DNA is then integrated into the host chromosome as a provirus.

✎ Quick Check 2.2

Which virus is the exception to the rule that DNA viruses replicate in the nucleus?

Poxvirus (which causes smallpox) is a dsDNA virus that uniquely replicates entirely within the CYTOPLASM. It carries its own DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, making it independent of the host nucleus.

The Viral Replication Cycle

①
Attachment (Adsorption) — Viral surface proteins bind specifically to complementary receptors on the host cell surface. This determines host specificity.
e.g. HIV gp120 protein binds CD4 receptor on T-helper cells
②
Penetration (Entry) — The virus or its nucleic acid enters the host cell. Enveloped viruses fuse their envelope with the cell membrane; non-enveloped viruses inject their genome or enter via endocytosis.
Bacteriophages inject DNA while leaving their capsid outside
③
Uncoating — The capsid is disassembled inside the host cell, releasing the viral nucleic acid.
④
Biosynthesis (Replication) — The viral genome is replicated and viral proteins (capsid proteins, enzymes) are synthesised using host cell ribosomes and resources.
The host cell is essentially converted into a virus factory
⑤
Assembly (Maturation) — New viral components (genome + capsid proteins) are assembled into complete virions.
⑥
Release (Lysis or Budding) — New virions exit the host cell. Non-enveloped viruses burst the cell open (lysis). Enveloped viruses bud off, acquiring their envelope from the host cell membrane.
Lysis kills the cell; budding may allow continued host cell survival temporarily

✎ Quick Check 3.1

Hepatitis A virus is most likely transmitted via which route?

Hepatitis A is transmitted via the fecal-oral route — through ingestion of contaminated food or water. Hepatitis B (not A) is transmitted via blood transfusion and sexual contact.

✎ Quick Check 3.2

During which step of viral replication does the virus use host ribosomes to produce viral proteins?

Biosynthesis (replication phase) is when the viral genome is copied and viral proteins are synthesised using the host cell's ribosomes, tRNA, amino acids, and energy. This is why viruses are obligate intracellular parasites.
§ 4 — Bacteria

Characteristics of Bacteria

Bacteria are microscopic, single-celled prokaryotic organisms that cannot be seen with the naked eye. Unlike viruses, bacteria are unambiguously living — they metabolise, grow, and reproduce independently. They are the most abundant organisms on Earth and occupy every conceivable habitat. Their cell wall composition and morphology are the foundations for identification and classification.

Core Definition
Bacteria are unicellular prokaryotes with: no true nucleus (DNA in nucleoid region), no membrane-bound organelles, a peptidoglycan cell wall, and reproduction by binary fission. They are typically 0.5–5 µm in size.

The 11 Key Characteristics

1. Unicellular
Made up of a single cell that carries out all life functions.
2. Prokaryotic
Lack a true membrane-bound nucleus. DNA exists freely in the nucleoid region of the cytoplasm.
3. No Membrane-Bound Organelles
Lack mitochondria, ER, Golgi apparatus. Ribosomes (70S) are present but are smaller than eukaryotic ribosomes (80S).
This difference is targeted by many antibiotics (e.g. streptomycin)
4. Peptidoglycan Cell Wall
All bacteria have cell walls made of peptidoglycan (murein). The thickness and structure differ between Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria.
5. Microscopic Size
Typically 0.5–5 µm. Can be seen under a light microscope (unlike viruses, which need electron microscopy).
6. Various Shapes
Cocci (spherical), Bacilli (rod), Spirilla (spiral), Vibrios (comma-shaped). Shape is a primary identification tool.
7. Binary Fission
Asexual reproduction by splitting into two identical daughter cells. Under ideal conditions, can divide every 20 minutes.
8. Motility
Some are motile using flagella (whip-like projections), pili, or gliding. Motility helps bacteria chemotaxis toward nutrients or away from toxins.
9. Autotrophic & Heterotrophic
Some (autotrophs) make their own food via photosynthesis or chemosynthesis. Others (heterotrophs) obtain nutrients from organic matter.
10. Ubiquitous
Found almost everywhere: soil, water, air, deep ocean vents, hot springs, and inside other organisms. Highly adaptable to extreme environments.
11. Beneficial & Pathogenic
Many are essential for ecosystem function (nitrogen fixation, decomposition). Others cause serious diseases.
🎉 Fun Fact

The human body contains approximately 38 trillion bacterial cells — slightly more than the 30 trillion human cells! The gut alone hosts over 1,000 species of bacteria that aid digestion, synthesise vitamins, and protect against pathogens.

✎ Quick Check 4.1

Which feature is COMMON to both viruses and bacteria?

Both viruses and bacteria contain nucleic acid (genetic material). However: bacteria have BOTH DNA and RNA; viruses have one or the other. Bacteria have peptidoglycan walls, ribosomes, and cytoplasm — viruses have none of these.

2. Staining Techniques

Gram Staining
The most important staining technique. Differentiates bacteria based on cell wall thickness. Uses crystal violet, iodine, alcohol wash, and safranin counterstain.
Gram+ = purple (thick peptidoglycan) · Gram− = pink (thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane)
Acid-Fast Staining (Ziehl-Neelsen)
Detects acid-fast bacteria — those with waxy mycolic acid in their cell walls that resist decolourisation by acid-alcohol. Result: bright red on blue background.
Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB), M. leprae (leprosy)
Endospore Staining (Schaeffer-Fulton)
Detects heat-resistant endospores formed by certain bacteria under harsh conditions. Malachite green + steam drives dye into spores; safranin counterstains vegetative cells.
Clostridium tetani (tetanus), Bacillus anthracis (anthrax)
Gram Staining Steps — Must Memorise
1. Apply Crystal Violet (primary stain) → 2. Apply Iodine (mordant — fixes stain) → 3. Decolourisation with Alcohol/Acetone → 4. Counterstain with Safranin

Gram-positive: Thick peptidoglycan retains crystal violet → Purple
Gram-negative: Thin peptidoglycan + outer lipid membrane loses crystal violet after wash → takes up safranin → Pink/Red

3. Biochemical Tests

TestWhat It DetectsPositive Result Indicates

4. Molecular Methods

DNA Sequencing
Determines the exact nucleotide sequence of bacterial DNA. Gold standard for definitive identification and phylogenetic classification.
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)
Amplifies specific DNA sequences for detection. Extremely sensitive — can detect a single bacterium. Used for rapid clinical diagnosis.
COVID-19 PCR tests use this same principle
16S rRNA Analysis
The 16S ribosomal RNA gene is highly conserved in bacteria but contains variable regions unique to each species. Sequencing these regions allows precise species identification.

5. Culture Characteristics

Growing bacteria on specific agar media reveals colony characteristics that aid identification: colony colour, size, edge morphology (smooth, irregular, filamentous), texture (mucoid, dry), and haemolysis patterns on blood agar.

📋 Key Media Examples: MacConkey agar (selects Gram-negative, differentiates lactose fermenters) · Blood agar (shows haemolysis patterns) · Chocolate agar (for fastidious organisms like Neisseria, Haemophilus) · Mannitol Salt Agar (selective for Staphylococcus)

✎ Quick Check 5.1

In Gram staining, why do Gram-positive bacteria appear purple?

Gram-positive bacteria have a THICK peptidoglycan cell wall that traps the crystal violet-iodine complex even after the alcohol wash. Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer but an outer lipid membrane — the alcohol dissolves this membrane and washes out the crystal violet, so they take up the safranin counterstain (pink).

✎ Quick Check 5.2

The catalase test differentiates which two clinically important bacteria?

The catalase test produces bubbles (O₂ gas) when hydrogen peroxide is added. Staphylococcus is catalase-positive (bubbles); Streptococcus is catalase-negative (no bubbles). Both are Gram-positive cocci, so this test is crucial for telling them apart clinically.

Can grow with or without oxygen. Prefer oxygen but can switch to fermentation when oxygen is absent. Most important pathogens are facultative anaerobes.

Microaerophiles
Require oxygen but at lower concentrations than atmospheric (21%). Damaged by normal oxygen levels.
Helicobacter pylori, Campylobacter jejuni

✎ Quick Check 6.1

Clostridium tetani causes tetanus. Which combination of features best describes this organism?

Clostridium tetani is Gram-positive, rod-shaped (bacillus), and strictly anaerobic. It thrives in deep puncture wounds devoid of oxygen. It produces the tetanospasmin toxin that causes the "lockjaw" spastic paralysis associated with tetanus.

✎ Quick Check 6.2

Which bacterium is responsible for nitrogen fixation in the root nodules of leguminous plants?

Rhizobium forms symbiotic relationships with leguminous plants and lives in root nodules, fixing atmospheric N₂ into ammonia (NH₃). Nitrosomonas converts NH₃ → NO₂⁻; Nitrobacter converts NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻. These are separate, sequential steps in soil nitrification.
§ 7 — Fungi

Characteristics of Fungi

Fungi are a unique kingdom of non-green, eukaryotic organisms that obtain food by absorbing nutrients from organic matter — they are heterotrophic decomposers. Unlike plants, they have no chlorophyll and cannot photosynthesise. Unlike animals, they have rigid cell walls. The study of fungi is called mycology. Fungi include moulds, yeasts, and mushrooms — three very different-looking organisms that share the same fundamental biology.

Core Definition
Fungi are eukaryotic, heterotrophic, non-photosynthetic organisms with chitin cell walls. They are mostly multicellular (except yeast) and reproduce via spores. They obtain nutrition by absorption of organic matter (saprophytic or parasitic).

The 9 Key Characteristics

1. Eukaryotic
Have a true membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (unlike bacteria). This is a fundamental distinction from bacteria.
2. Non-Photosynthetic
Lack chlorophyll entirely. Cannot capture sunlight to make food — must obtain energy from organic sources.
3. Heterotrophic
Obtain energy by breaking down and absorbing organic matter. Can be saprophytic (feed on dead matter) or parasitic (feed on living hosts).
4. Chitin Cell Wall
The cell wall is made of chitin — a tough polysaccharide also found in insect exoskeletons. This distinguishes fungi from plants (cellulose) and bacteria (peptidoglycan).
5. Hyphae & Mycelium
The fungal body is made of thread-like structures called hyphae. A mass of hyphae forms a mycelium. Hyphae may be septate (with cross walls, e.g., Penicillium) or aseptate/coenocytic (without cross walls, e.g., Mucor).
6. Reproduction by Spores
Produced on spherical sporangia borne on long stalks called sporangiophores. Spores can be sexual or asexual. Highly resistant to harsh conditions.
7. Mostly Multicellular
Multicellular except for yeasts, which are unicellular. Yeasts reproduce by budding, not hyphae.
8. Store Food as Glycogen
Store reserve carbohydrates as glycogen — like animals. Plants store starch; bacteria store various forms. This biochemical fact points to the evolutionary link between fungi and animals.
9. Thrive in Warm, Damp Conditions
Prefer warm, moist, dark environments. This explains why fungal infections (tinea, ringworm, thrush) are more common in humid regions and sweaty areas of the body.
🎉 Fun Fact

The largest living organism on Earth is a fungus! Armillaria ostoyae (honey fungus) in Oregon, USA, covers over 2,385 acres (9.65 km²) of soil and is estimated to be 8,650 years old. Most of it is invisible — underground mycelium.

✎ Quick Check 7.1

Which of the following correctly distinguishes fungi from bacteria?

The key distinction: Fungi are EUKARYOTIC (true nucleus, membrane-bound organelles); Bacteria are PROKARYOTIC (no true nucleus). Also: Fungi have chitin cell walls; bacteria have peptidoglycan. Fungi store glycogen; neither have chlorophyll. Fungi reproduce by spores; bacteria by binary fission.

Normally found in small amounts in the human gut and vagina. Overgrowth causes candidiasis (oral thrush, vaginal yeast infection). Particularly dangerous in immunocompromised patients (HIV/AIDS).

The iconic red-and-white spotted toadstool. Highly poisonous — contains muscimol and ibotenic acid. Associated with fairy tales but should never be consumed.

✎ Quick Check 8.1

Penicillin is derived from which fungus?

Alexander Fleming discovered in 1928 that Penicillium notatum mould secreted a substance (penicillin) that killed surrounding Staphylococcus bacteria. This accidental discovery revolutionised medicine. Aspergillus niger produces citric acid, not penicillin.

✎ Quick Check 8.2

Fungi classified as Deuteromycota (imperfect fungi) are characterised by:

Deuteromycota = "imperfect fungi" because their sexual stage is unknown or absent. They reproduce only asexually by producing conidia. Once a sexual stage is discovered, they are reclassified into Ascomycota or Basidiomycota. Candida albicans is the most clinically important example.
§ 9 — The Big Picture

Viruses vs. Bacteria vs. Fungi: The Master Comparison

This is the most tested area in BIO 102 examinations. The ability to compare and contrast these three groups across multiple features is essential.

🦠 Viruses
  • Non-living, acellular
  • DNA or RNA (never both)
  • No cell wall
  • Capsid (+ envelope in some)
  • Obligate intracellular parasite
  • No independent metabolism
  • Replicate (not reproduce)
  • 20–300 nm (ultramicroscopic)
  • Host specific
  • Treated with antivirals
🔬 Bacteria
  • Living, unicellular
  • Both DNA and RNA
  • Peptidoglycan cell wall
  • Prokaryotic (no true nucleus)
  • Independent reproduction
  • Binary fission
  • 0.5–5 µm (microscopic)
  • Can be aerobic/anaerobic
  • Can be beneficial or pathogenic
  • Treated with antibiotics
🍄 Fungi
  • Living, mostly multicellular
  • Both DNA and RNA
  • Chitin cell wall
  • Eukaryotic (true nucleus)
  • Independent reproduction
  • Spores (+ budding in yeast)
  • Macroscopic to microscopic
  • Heterotrophic (absorptive)
  • Can be beneficial or pathogenic
  • Treated with antifungals

✎ Quick Check 9.1

A patient's biopsy reveals a eukaryotic organism with chitin in its cell wall and septate hyphae. Which organism is this most likely?

Eukaryotic + chitin cell wall + septate hyphae = FUNGUS. Specifically, septate hyphae (hyphae with cross walls between cells) are characteristic of Ascomycota (e.g., Penicillium, Aspergillus) and Basidiomycota. Aseptate hyphae characterise Zygomycota.

✎ Quick Check 9.2

Which statement correctly distinguishes viruses from bacteria?

A critical distinction: Viruses have EITHER DNA or RNA (not both); bacteria have both DNA and RNA. Antibiotics work on bacteria (target peptidoglycan synthesis, ribosome function) but NOT on viruses. Viruses cannot reproduce independently — they are obligate intracellular parasites.
§ 10 — Final Exam

Final MCQ Examination

Test your complete understanding of BIO 102. These exam-style questions cover all three topics — viruses, bacteria, and fungi — at the depth expected in university examinations.

✎ Final Q1

The capsomere is best defined as:

Capsomeres are individual protein subunits that assemble together to form the capsid (protein coat). Multiple capsomeres arranged in icosahedral or helical patterns create the characteristic capsid shapes.

✎ Final Q2

A negative-sense (−) RNA virus needs which molecule to begin protein synthesis?

Negative-sense (−) RNA cannot be directly translated. The virus must carry its own RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) to convert (−) RNA → (+) RNA, which can then serve as mRNA. Reverse transcriptase converts RNA → DNA (retroviruses only).

✎ Final Q3

Vibrio cholerae, the causative agent of cholera, is best classified as:

Vibrio cholerae is Gram-negative and comma-shaped (the "vibrio" shape is named after this organism). It causes cholera through the production of cholera toxin, leading to massive water and electrolyte loss (rice-water diarrhoea).

✎ Final Q4

Which of the following is the study of fungi called?

Mycology (from Greek: mykes = fungus) is the study of fungi. Bacteriology = bacteria; Virology = viruses; Parasitology = parasites; Epidemiology = disease patterns in populations.

✎ Final Q5

Bacteriophages infect bacteria. What is their shape most accurately described as?

T4 bacteriophages (the classic phage) have a COMPLEX structure: an icosahedral head (containing DNA) attached to a helical tail with tail fibres. This combined/radial symmetry is categorised as complex morphology — making them the most architecturally sophisticated viruses.

✎ Final Q6

A student observes aseptate hyphae with sporangiospores in a bread sample. Which fungus is this most likely?

Rhizopus stolonifer (bread mould, Zygomycota) has aseptate hyphae (no cross walls) and produces sporangiospores in spherical sporangia. Penicillium has septate hyphae and produces conidia. Candida is a yeast (unicellular). Agaricus is a mushroom (macrofungus).

✎ Final Q7

Which of the following correctly pairs a bacterial disease with its causative organism?

Tetanus IS caused by Clostridium tetani ✓. Correct pairings: Cholera → Vibrio cholerae; Tuberculosis → Mycobacterium tuberculosis; Typhoid fever → Salmonella typhi; Whooping cough → Bordetella pertussis.

✎ Final Q8

Fungi store carbohydrates as:

Fungi store food as GLYCOGEN — the same storage carbohydrate used by animals! This is one of several features suggesting fungi and animals share a more recent common ancestor than fungi and plants. Plants store starch; bacteria store various forms including poly-β-hydroxybutyrate.

✎ Final Q9

Which mode of viral transmission is associated with Rabies virus?

Rabies is a zoonosis transmitted via the bite (or scratch) of an infected animal — classically a dog, bat, fox, or raccoon. The virus travels along peripheral nerves to the brain. Once symptoms appear, it is almost universally fatal — making post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) critical.

✎ Final Q10

Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB) is identified using which staining technique?

M. tuberculosis is ACID-FAST because its cell wall contains large amounts of mycolic acid (a waxy lipid) that resists decolourisation by acid-alcohol. In the Ziehl-Neelsen stain, acid-fast bacteria appear bright red/pink against a blue background. It is neither Gram-positive nor Gram-negative by the classical Gram stain.
